\begin{abstract}

 What role should exploration take in GRPO-style reasoning RL? Existing exploration mechanisms act either by (1) scaling entire prompts with uncertainty estimates or by (2) increasing token-level stochasticity. Yet, under GRPO, RL learns from groups: for each prompt, the policy samples several completions and the update is driven by comparisons inside that prompt-local set. To align exploration with model update, we introduce \emph{\method}, a maximum-entropy variant of \drgrpo that places exploration directly on the sampled candidate distribution. For each prompt, \method constructs a target distribution over completions by combining reward-induced group fit with candidate-level Shannon entropy. The resulting target has a closed form and gives a clean way to separate prompt-level, token-level, and candidate-level regularization. We evaluate this design with a matched multi-seed regression analysis on two synthetic exact multi-answer benchmarks --- Countdown arithmetic and graph-coloring completion --- in which every prompt admits several verifiably distinct valid answers, using aggregate accuracy and answer-mode coverage as descriptive evidence and regression coefficients to estimate effect size and uncertainty across task domains, training seeds, and evaluation seeds. At the 0.5B scale, the pre-registered primary temperature is null, but exploratory aggressive temperatures show the pre-registered ordered signature of candidate-level exploration: $+4.8$ points of answer-mode coverage and $+7.9$ points \texttt{pass@8} (Holm-adjusted $p<10^{-4}$) at statistically flat per-sample accuracy, while prompt-level uncertainty scaling matches the baseline and token-level entropy regularization is harmful.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning (RL) has become a central component in the post-training for large language models (LLMs), especially for reasoning tasks where the desired behavior cannot be fully specified by supervised imitation alone \citep{ouyang2022training,shao2024deepseekmath,guo2025deepseekr1}. Verifiable domains such as mathematics and code are particularly natural testbeds: candidate solutions can often be checked automatically, and recent work increasingly uses outcome or process feedback to improve reasoning \citep{hendrycks2021math,cobbe2021training,lewkowycz2022solving,lightman2023lets,le2022coderl}. In these settings, learning depends not only on rewarding successful answers but also on whether training produces useful comparisons among alternative reasoning paths. The central question is therefore: how should an RL fine-tuning algorithm encourage a model to generate a useful group of reasoning candidates at each training step?

This question holds particular significance for Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). GRPO-style methods sample multiple completions for the same prompt, compute rewards within that prompt-local group, and update the policy from the resulting relative signal \citep{shao2024deepseekmath,liu2025understanding}. The model does not learn from a single completion in isolation but rather from the structure of a sampled set. Exploration is therefore critical in  two respects: it determines which reasoning paths are available for comparison, and it affects how informative the resulting group-relative update can be. Prior work on self-consistency and related sampling methods shows that diverse reasoning paths can materially improve reasoning performance, suggesting that useful alternatives matter even when only one final answer is selected \citep{wang2023selfconsistency,lin2025cppo}.

A mismatch arises because the supervision in GRPO is groupwise, while most exploration mechanisms are not. Some methods act at the prompt level, for example by using semantic uncertainty to scale the update for an entire question \citep{chen2025seedgrpo,farquhar2024detecting}. Others act at the token level, for example by adding a next-token entropy bonus to the loss \citep{williams1991entropy,mnih2016a3c} or by entropy-regularized token-level objectives for language agents \citep{wen2024etpo}. Both can be useful, but neither directly regularizes the prompt-local distribution over sampled completions, which is the object GRPO uses to assign credit. Raising sampling temperature can increase surface-level variation, but it does not ensure that the group contains meaningfully different or useful reasoning paths. This strategy can just as easily create redundant or incorrect strategies, noisy off-policy completions, or higher-variance updates.

We propose \textbf{\method}, a candidate-level maximum-entropy formulation built on the \drgrpo backbone \citep{liu2025understanding}. The core idea is simple: if GRPO learns from a group, exploration should be expressed on that same group. For each prompt, \method constructs a distribution over the sampled completions. This distribution balances two forces: higher probability for higher-reward candidates and Shannon entropy over the candidate set (an optional reference tilt toward the base model is analyzed in Appendix~\ref{app:reference-tilt} but not used in our experiments). The resulting weights have a closed form, are computed once per rollout batch and frozen for the update exactly as advantages are, and preserve compatibility with the standard prompt-group rollout pipeline: only the aggregation weights of the \drgrpo surrogate change.

Our evaluation is designed to match this conceptual claim. Rather than relying solely on pooled accuracy tables or best-checkpoint summaries, we evaluate the method with a regression-based analysis over matched runs. Each observation corresponds to a method, training seed, evaluation seed, task domain, prompt, and sampled completion or prompt-level aggregate. The coefficient on the \method indicator estimates the accuracy and coverage lift relative to \drgrpo after controlling for task-domain and seed effects. This approach allows us to report not only whether \method improves aggregate performance, but also the size of the estimated effect and the remaining uncertainty.

Our contributions are as follows. \textbf{(i)} We identify a structural mismatch in GRPO-style exploration: the learning signal is formed over sampled completion groups, while common regularizers act at the prompt or token level. \textbf{(ii)} We propose a groupwise maximum-entropy objective that regularizes the prompt-local candidate distribution and derive its closed-form target. \textbf{(iii)} We instantiate the objective as \method, a \drgrpo-based policy optimization method that can be compared against scalar group-relative updates, semantic-entropy scaling, and token-level entropy regularization under a shared backbone. \textbf{(iv)} We give the objective a KL-proximal interpretation and provide robustness guarantees. \textbf{(v)} We evaluate the method with a matched multi-seed regression protocol on two synthetic exact multi-answer benchmarks --- Countdown arithmetic and graph-coloring completion --- whose prompts each admit several verifiably distinct valid answers, making effect size and statistical uncertainty explicit.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\textbf{KL-regularized and maximum-entropy policy optimization.}
A broad line of work views policy optimization through KL-regularized or mirror-descent updates. Classical examples include REPS, MPO/V-MPO, MDPO, TRPO, and PPO, which use relative-entropy or trust-region constraints to stabilize policy improvement \citep{reps,mpo,vmpo,mdpo,trpo,schulman2017ppo}. This perspective has been formalized in regularized RL and policy mirror-descent frameworks \citep{geist2019regmdp,zhan2023pmd,vieillard2020kl}. Maximum-entropy RL adds an explicit entropy term to the control objective, yielding softened or Boltzmann-style solutions and connecting RL to control-as-inference and KL-control \citep{haarnoja18b,levine2018control,ziebart2008maximum,todorov2006}. Two closer relatives deserve note. Exponential advantage weighting of the form $w\propto\exp(A/\tau)$ is the E-step of reward-weighted and advantage-weighted regression \citep{peters2007rwr,peng2019awr}, and the log-sum-exp aggregation our objective reduces to is the \emph{entropic risk measure} of risk-sensitive control \citep{howard1972risk,mihatsch2002risk}. Our formulation builds on this tradition, but applies the exponential weights and the entropy term directly to a \emph{finite set of sampled candidates} within a prompt group, rather than to the full token-level action distribution or to a replay buffer of trajectories.

\textbf{Preference optimization and groupwise supervision for language models.}
Recent post-training methods for language models often optimize KL-regularized objectives induced by preferences or rankings. DPO, SimPO, ORPO, and KTO recast alignment as preference optimization without relying on standard on-policy RL pipelines \citep{dpo,simpo,orpo,kto}. In parallel, group methods such as LiPO and PRO emphasize supervision over sets of candidate responses rather than isolated pairwise comparisons \citep{liu2024lipo,song2024pro}. \method is complementary to these approaches: it retains an on-policy GRPO-style training loop, but introduces a prompt-local target distribution over sampled completions and regularizes that distribution directly.

\textbf{GRPO and its variants.}
GRPO is a critic-free, PPO-style post-training method that samples a group of completions, forms group-relative learning signals, and broadcasts them across tokens within each completion \citep{shao2024deepseekmath}. Dr.GRPO revisits this update and motivates a corrected backbone that reduces optimization pathologies in R1-Zero-style training \citep{liu2025understanding}. Several recent variants then modify where exploration or uncertainty enters the GRPO pipeline. SEED-GRPO uses semantic entropy to scale updates at the prompt level \citep{chen2025seedgrpo,farquhar2024detecting}, Info-GRPO introduces diversity through latent conditioning \citep{infogrpo,oord2018cpc}, and token-level entropy interventions range from the classic loss-side entropy bonus \citep{williams1991entropy,mnih2016a3c} to soft-Bellman objectives that carry a KL-to-reference regularizer inside the token-level backup \citep{wen2024etpo}. Our method differs in where it intervenes: it regularizes the \emph{distribution over sampled candidates} itself, yielding a closed-form prompt-local target and a direct candidate-level view of exploration in GRPO-style reasoning RL.

\section{Where Exploration Enters the Objective}
\label{sec:where-exploration}

GRPO-style methods learn from a \emph{group}. For each prompt, the policy samples
several completions, scores them, and forms a within-prompt comparison. Our claim is
that this group is also the natural place for exploration to act. We first observe that Dr.GRPO already
contains an aggregation step, and that this step is a hidden, unexamined design choice.

\paragraph{Common prompt-group setup.}
For each prompt $x$, the rollout policy $\mu$ samples $G$ completions
$\{y_{x,1},\dots,y_{x,G}\}$ with scalar rewards $r_{x,i}$, group mean
$\bar r_x = \tfrac1G\sum_i r_{x,i}$, and centered advantages $A_{x,i} = r_{x,i}-\bar r_x$.
Let $m_{x,i,t}$ be the completion-token mask, $c_{x,i,t}$ the tokenwise importance ratio
between $\pi_\theta$ and $\mu$, and $\tilde c_{x,i,t}$ its clipped version
(Appendix~\ref{app:common-rollout}). Define the \emph{per-candidate Dr.GRPO utility}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:utility}
\Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)
\;:=\;
\frac{1}{T_{\max}}
\sum_{t}
m_{x,i,t}\,
\min\!\bigl(
c_{x,i,t}A_{x,i},\;
\tilde c_{x,i,t}A_{x,i}
\bigr),
\end{equation}
where the constant $T_{\max}$ normalizer is exactly Dr.GRPO's length-bias correction
\citep{liu2025understanding}. Since $|A_{x,i}|\le 1$ for binary verifiable rewards and the
mask has at most $T_{\max}$ active positions, $|\Ubar_{x,i}|\le 1$ up to the clip range.

\paragraph{Dr.GRPO is a risk-neutral aggregator.}
Written this way, the Dr.GRPO objective is, up to a constant,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:drgrpo-as-uniform}
\mathcal J_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO}}(\theta)
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal A|}\sum_{x\in\mathcal A}
\;\Bigl\langle\, \mathbf{u},\; \Ubar_{x}(\theta) \,\Bigr\rangle,
\qquad
\mathbf{u} = \bigl(\tfrac1G,\dots,\tfrac1G\bigr)\in\Delta^G .
\end{equation}
That is: Dr.GRPO builds a group, computes a utility for each candidate, and then
\emph{averages them uniformly}. The group-relative signal lives entirely inside
$A_{x,i}$; the aggregation across candidates is a plain mean. Nothing in the derivation
of Dr.GRPO requires this. The uniform weights are an implicit choice, and they are the
choice that treats every sampled candidate as equally worth learning from.

\paragraph{xDr.GRPO: optimistic aggregation over the candidate group.}
xDr.GRPO replaces $\mathbf{u}$ by a prompt-local distribution $w_x\in\Delta^G$ chosen
variationally. For $\tau>0$ and $\lambda\ge 0$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xdr-objective}
\mathcal G_x(\theta)
\;:=\;
\max_{w\in\Delta^G}
\Bigl\{
\underbrace{\langle w, \Ubar_x(\theta)\rangle}_{\text{same Dr.GRPO utilities}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\tau H(w)}_{\text{candidate-level entropy}}
\;-\;
\underbrace{\lambda\,\KL\!\bigl(w\,\|\,\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x\bigr)}_{\text{reference tilt}}
\Bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x\in\Delta^G$ is a reference-induced distribution over the same
sampled set (Appendix~\ref{app:reference-tilt}), and the training objective is
$\mathcal J_{\mathrm{xDr.GRPO}}(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal A|}\sum_{x\in\mathcal A}\mathcal G_x(\theta)$.
The maximizer is a tilted softmax over utilities,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xdr-closed-form}
w^\star_{x,i}(\theta)
=
\frac{
\exp\!\bigl(\Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)/(\tau+\lambda)\bigr)\,
\bigl(\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,i}\bigr)^{\lambda/(\tau+\lambda)}
}{
\sum_{j=1}^G
\exp\!\bigl(\Ubar_{x,j}(\theta)/(\tau+\lambda)\bigr)\,
\bigl(\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,j}\bigr)^{\lambda/(\tau+\lambda)}
},
\end{equation}
and eliminating $w$ leaves a log-partition (soft-max) objective
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xdr-lse}
\mathcal G_x(\theta)
=
(\tau+\lambda)\,
\log\!\sum_{i=1}^G
\exp\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)}{\tau+\lambda}\Bigr)
\bigl(\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,i}\bigr)^{\lambda/(\tau+\lambda)} .
\end{equation}
Proofs are in Appendix~\ref{app:theory}. Crucially, by the envelope theorem the gradient
is the \emph{same per-candidate Dr.GRPO gradient, reweighted}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xdr-gradient}
\nabla_\theta \mathcal G_x(\theta)
=
\sum_{i=1}^G w^\star_{x,i}(\theta)\,\nabla_\theta \Ubar_{x,i}(\theta),
\end{equation}
with $w^\star$ entering as a constant: the implementation computes the weights once per
rollout batch at the rollout policy --- where the clipped utility reduces to the closed form
$A_{x,i}T_{x,i}/T_{\max}$ --- and freezes them for the update, exactly as the advantages are
frozen (Remark~\ref{rem:frozen-weights}). The rollout
pipeline, reward function, clipping, and tokenwise surrogate are untouched. \emph{Only the
aggregation weights change.}

\paragraph{Why this is exploration, and why Dr.GRPO has none.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:xdr-lse} is a tempered log-sum-exp: the \emph{entropic risk measure} of
the candidate utilities \citep{howard1972risk,mihatsch2002risk}, with $\tau+\lambda$ as
inverse risk-sensitivity. Its second-order
expansion (Proposition~\ref{prop:variance-expansion}) makes the mechanism explicit:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:variance-expansion-main}
\mathcal G_x(\theta)
=
\underbrace{\tau\log G}_{\text{constant}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^G \Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)}_{\textbf{Dr.GRPO}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\frac{1}{2\tau}\,\Var_{i}\!\bigl(\Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)\bigr)}_{\textbf{group-level exploration bonus}}
\;+\;
O(\tau^{-2})
\qquad (\lambda=0).
\end{equation}
Three consequences follow, and they replace the informal ``entropy adds stochasticity''
story used by prior work.

\emph{(i) Dr.GRPO is the risk-neutral member of this family.} As $\tau\to\infty$,
$w^\star\to\mathbf{u}$ and $\nabla_\theta\mathcal G_x\to\nabla_\theta\mathcal J_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO},x}$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:drgrpo-limit}). The baseline is not ``xDr.GRPO without
exploration''; it is \emph{xDr.GRPO at zero risk-sensitivity}. Exploration is therefore not
an additive gadget bolted onto Dr.GRPO --- it is a parameter Dr.GRPO silently pins at a
corner.

\emph{(ii) Finite $\tau$ is optimism over the group.} By Jensen,
$\mathcal G_x - \tau\log G \ge \mathcal J_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO},x}$, with the gap equal to the
within-group \emph{dispersion} of candidate utilities. xDr.GRPO thus prefers parameters that
keep the sampled group spread out in utility --- that is, prompts and policies where the
group still disagrees. This is optimism in the face of uncertainty, expressed on the object
GRPO uses for credit assignment, rather than on the prompt (SEED-Dr.GRPO) or the next-token
distribution (Token-MaxEnt Dr.GRPO).

\emph{(iii) The mechanism is asymmetric gradient attenuation.} Because
$w^\star_{x,i}\propto \exp(\Ubar_{x,i}/\tau)$ and $\Ubar_{x,i}$ is increasing in $A_{x,i}$,
negative-advantage candidates receive \emph{exponentially downweighted} gradients. How such
attenuation interacts with policy-entropy dynamics under verifiable rewards is genuinely
contested: entropy collapse has been traced to high-covariance updates on confident
\emph{positive}-advantage tokens \citep{cui2025entropy}, and sequence-level negative
reinforcement alone can even preserve entropy \citep{zhu2025negative}, while negative
gradients applied to \emph{low-probability} tokens are a documented entropy-collapsing and
likelihood-displacing force whose selective attenuation helps
\citep{park2025cliplow,huang2025lowprob,deng2025negativegrad}. Our reweighting acts at the
candidate level rather than the token level, so we treat its net entropy effect as an
empirical question --- Figure~\ref{fig:exploration-sidecar} measures it directly through the
effective number of active rollouts and the incorrect-mass share, logged identically for
every arm.

\paragraph{The family, end to end.}
The temperature interpolates between two known regimes:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
$\tau \to 0$ & $w^\star \to \mathbf{1}\{i = \argmax_i \Ubar_{x,i}\}$ & best-of-$G$ distillation (RAFT-style, \citealp{dong2023raft}) \\
$0 < \tau < \infty$ & tilted softmax over utilities & \textbf{xDr.GRPO} \\
$\tau \to \infty$ & $w^\star \to \mathbf{u}$ & \textbf{Dr.GRPO} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Our empirical claim is that the optimum lies in the \emph{interior}: rejection-sampling-style
concentration discards the comparative signal that makes GRPO work, while uniform
aggregation spends a fixed share of every update on negative candidates --- whether that
degrades the sampled group is exactly what the diagnostics of
Figure~\ref{fig:exploration-sidecar} test. Section~\ref{sec:experimental-design}
tests this with a sweep over $\tau$ in which Dr.GRPO appears as the $\tau=\infty$ endpoint
of the same family, run through the same launcher with the reweighting disabled
(Remark~\ref{rem:frozen-weights}).

\paragraph{Degenerate groups need no special handling.}
If all sampled rewards are equal, then $A_{x,i}=0$ for all $i$, hence $\Ubar_{x,i}=0$,
$w^\star_x$ is uniform (or reference-tilted), and $\nabla_\theta\mathcal G_x=0$. Uninformative
prompts contribute no gradient automatically, matching Dr.GRPO's behavior; no
prompt-skipping heuristic is required.

\section{Compared Methods}
\label{sec:quartet-methods}

We organize the comparison around where exploration enters an otherwise shared GRPO-style training pipeline. The rollout interface, reward function, base model, reference model, and optimization budget are held fixed. The methods differ only in the ways they transform a prompt-local group of sampled completions into an update.

\paragraph{\drgrpo.}
\drgrpo is the scalar-weight baseline. It centers rewards within each prompt group, assigns each sampled completion a scalar advantage, and applies the clipped \drgrpo surrogate \citep{shao2024deepseekmath,liu2025understanding}. It therefore uses group-relative information, but collapses the group before optimization.

\paragraph{\method.}
\method is the candidate-level variant. It constructs a prompt-local weight distribution over the sampled completions and reweights each candidate's \drgrpo update by it. This method directly regularizes the object being compared inside GRPO: the set of sampled candidates for a prompt.

\paragraph{\tokmaxdrgrpo.}
Token-level MaxEnt keeps the \drgrpo backbone but adds the classic entropy bonus to the next-token objective \citep{williams1991entropy,mnih2016a3c}, encouraging locally higher-entropy token distributions during optimization; entropy-regularized token-level objectives for language agents pursue the same intuition through a soft-Bellman formulation \citep{wen2024etpo}. This approach tests whether token-level stochasticity alone can provide the same benefit as candidate-level exploration.

\paragraph{\seeddrgrpo.}
SEED-style \drgrpo preserves the clipped \drgrpo update but rescales prompt updates using semantic uncertainty, following semantic-entropy approaches that estimate uncertainty over answer meaning rather than over individual token choices \citep{chen2025seedgrpo,farquhar2024detecting}. This technique thus tests a prompt-level intervention.

Together, these methods form a clean axis: no extra exploration beyond rollout sampling (\drgrpo), prompt-level uncertainty scaling (\seeddrgrpo), token-level entropy regularization (\tokmaxdrgrpo), and candidate-level distribution matching (\method). The main paired regression analysis focuses on the completed \drgrpo versus \method runs; the same regression specification extends directly to the full quartet by replacing the binary method indicator with method fixed effects.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental-design}

We evaluate \method in verifiable reasoning tasks with \emph{multiple valid solutions per prompt}. The testbed consists of two synthetic exact multi-answer domains, generated by exhaustive enumeration so that the full set of valid answers for every prompt is known.

\paragraph{Countdown arithmetic.}
Each prompt presents three distinct integers drawn from $\{2,\dots,12\}$ and an integer target, and asks the model to return a single arithmetic expression, inside \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{\}}, that reaches the target using each given number exactly once with $+$, $-$, $\times$, $/$, and parentheses \citep{gandhi2024stream,tinyzero2025}. All reachable expressions are enumerated exactly (every permutation of the numbers, every binary expression tree, rational arithmetic), and each expression is reduced to a canonical form in which commutative operands are sorted while operator identity is preserved. A prompt's \emph{answer modes} are its distinct canonical solution expressions: for example, the numbers $[2,3,4]$ reach the target $10$ through the two distinct modes $2\times 3+4$ and $3\times 4-2$.

\paragraph{Graph-coloring completion.}
Each prompt presents a small undirected graph (vertices $1$ through $n$), its edge list, and a partial coloring with hidden vertices, and asks the model to return colors from $\{1,2,3\}$ for the hidden vertices, boxed, such that no edge has equal colors at both endpoints. All valid colorings consistent with the partial assignment are enumerated exactly; a prompt's answer modes are its distinct valid completions.

In both domains, prompts are filtered so that every training and evaluation item admits several modes (between $2$ and $8$ for Countdown), which makes the pair a direct probe of whether a training objective finds \emph{more of the valid answer set} rather than one answer more reliably. The problem scale is calibrated so that the base policy earns reward often enough to bootstrap: three-number Countdown gives an untrained small model a workable success rate, whereas four-number variants yield almost no rewarded rollouts and hence no learning signal. The released configurations use $384$ training prompts and $128$ held-out evaluation prompts for Countdown ($192$/$96$ for graph coloring), generated deterministically from fixed seeds, plus optional single-mode control splits in which each prompt has exactly one valid answer. Evaluation prompts are excluded from the training pool by problem identity (numbers and target for Countdown; graph, edge set, and partial coloring for graph coloring), so no evaluation item is ever trained on. In the released Countdown configuration the evaluation split's mode counts concentrate at five modes per prompt (median $5$, range $2$--$8$) with targets in $[1,36]$. The primary paired comparison is \drgrpo versus \method, fine-tuning Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct policies with $G=16$ rollouts per prompt during training. For each method, we train three independent seeds per domain and evaluate each trained checkpoint with three evaluation seeds; evaluation draws $K=8$ sampled completions per prompt at temperature $1.0$ (independent of the training group size) plus one greedy completion, always from the final saved checkpoint --- a rule fixed before any evaluation was run.

All methods use the same base model, prompt format, reward extraction logic, rollout budget, and evaluation harness. Correctness is verifiable and binary: the boxed expression is normalized, parsed, and evaluated with exact rational arithmetic, and receives reward $1$ if and only if it equals the target and uses each given number exactly once. Mode identity is never part of the training reward --- the trainer sees correctness only --- so any difference in how many distinct modes a method discovers is attributable to the training objective rather than to mode-aware supervision.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

We compute five descriptive metrics. Greedy \texttt{pass@1} measures whether the deterministic completion is correct. Sampled \texttt{pass@8} measures whether at least one of eight sampled completions is correct for a prompt. Sampled \texttt{mean@8} measures the average correctness rate across the eight sampled completions. Because every prompt admits several distinct valid modes, we additionally track \texttt{distinct@8}, the number of distinct canonical answer modes among the correct sampled completions, and \texttt{coverage@8}, defined as \texttt{distinct@8} divided by the prompt's total number of valid modes. The coverage metrics measure exactly what candidate-level exploration is supposed to buy: discovering more of the valid answer set, not only finding one answer more often. These aggregate metrics are useful summaries, but they do not by themselves quantify uncertainty across difficulty strata, training seeds, and evaluation seeds. For that reason, our primary statistical comparison is regression-based.

\subsection{Regression-Based Evaluation}
\label{sec:regression-evaluation}

For each method $m$, training seed $s$, evaluation seed $e$, task domain $b$ (Countdown or graph coloring), prompt $p$, and sampled completion $k$, let
\[
C_{m,s,e,b,p,k}\in\{0,1\}
\]
indicate whether the normalized boxed answer is correct. We form prompt-level outcomes from these completion-level indicators:
\[
Y^{\texttt{pass@8}}_{m,s,e,b,p}
=
\1\!\left\{\max_{1\le k\le 8} C_{m,s,e,b,p,k}=1\right\},
\qquad
Y^{\texttt{mean@8}}_{m,s,e,b,p}
=
\frac{1}{8}\sum_{k=1}^8 C_{m,s,e,b,p,k}.
\]
For greedy \texttt{pass@1}, $Y^{\texttt{pass@1}}_{m,s,e,b,p}$ is the corresponding greedy correctness indicator. For coverage, let $d_{m,s,e,b,p}$ be the number of distinct canonical answer modes among the correct sampled completions and $M_{b,p}$ the prompt's known number of valid modes; then
\[
Y^{\texttt{coverage@8}}_{m,s,e,b,p} = \frac{d_{m,s,e,b,p}}{M_{b,p}} \in [0,1].
\]

For each outcome $Y$, we estimate the paired linear probability model
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:regression-main}
Y_{m,s,e,b,p}
=
\alpha
+\gamma\,\1\{m=\methodmath\}
+\delta_b
+\eta_s
+\zeta_e
+\varepsilon_{m,s,e,b,p}.
\end{equation}
Here $\delta_b$ are task-domain fixed effects, $\eta_s$ are training-seed fixed effects, and $\zeta_e$ are evaluation-seed fixed effects. The coefficient $\gamma$ is the estimated effect of \method relative to \drgrpo in accuracy points, after controlling for the matched evaluation design. We report $\hat\gamma$, its standard error, a confidence interval, and a $p$-value. Standard errors are clustered by evaluation prompt when prompt identities are shared across methods. We additionally check robustness to clustering by training run when reporting pooled results across task domains. Table~\ref{tab:regression-protocol}
summarizes the role of each regression component.

This regression analysis is the paper's main answer to the question ``is the observed improvement statistically distinguishable from seed and dataset variation?'' The raw accuracy tables show the size and direction of the observed differences. Equation~\ref{eq:regression-main} tests whether those differences persist after accounting for the repeated-seed evaluation structure.

\subsection{Power and Sample Size Planning}

The study is powered around the regression coefficient $\gamma$ in
Equation~3, which estimates the absolute accuracy lift from replacing
Dr.GRPO with xDr.GRPO. Power is assessed at the prompt level rather than
the completion level, since completions from the same prompt and repeated
evaluations of the same evaluation item are not independent. In the main
paired comparison, each method is trained with three independent fine-tuning
seeds and each checkpoint is evaluated with three stochastic evaluation
seeds, yielding up to $2 \times 3 \times 3$ method-run evaluations per
prompt across the two task domains. Before interpreting null effects as evidence
of no improvement, we will estimate the minimum detectable effect for each
outcome using the prompt-clustered standard error from the planned regression
or, equivalently, a cluster bootstrap that resamples prompts and training runs.

For a two-sided test with $\alpha = 0.05$ and 80\% power, the approximate
minimum detectable effect is
\[
\mathrm{MDE}
\approx
\left(z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{0.80}\right)
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\hat{\gamma})
=
(1.96 + 0.84)\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\hat{\gamma})
\approx
2.8\,\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\hat{\gamma}).
\]
Thus, if the clustered standard error for $\hat{\gamma}$ is $0.01$, the
design can detect an improvement of roughly $2.8$ percentage points; if it is
$0.02$, the detectable improvement is roughly $5.6$ percentage points. We
will treat an absolute gain of 3 to 5 points on \texttt{pass@8},
\texttt{mean@8}, or \texttt{coverage@8} as the smallest practically meaningful effect. If the
estimated MDE is larger than this range, the experiment will be reported as
underpowered and interpreted descriptively rather than as strong evidence for
or against xDr.GRPO. With $128$ evaluation prompts per domain and binary
prompt-level outcomes, realistic prompt-clustered standard errors fall in the
$0.013$--$0.027$ range, corresponding to minimum detectable effects of roughly
$4$--$8$ accuracy points; effects smaller than that are beyond this design's
resolution and will be treated as such.

\subsection{Interpreting the Current Run}
\label{sec:interpreting-current-run}

The current model scale is intentionally small, so we treat the experiment as a controlled test of the objective rather than a claim that group-level exploration already dominates at scale. A small positive aggregate difference can be meaningful only if it remains significant after the regression analysis above. Conversely, a non-significant coefficient would indicate that the present run does not yet separate the method effect from seed, dataset, and sampling noise.

The benchmark also makes the failure mode we target directly visible, which yields an \emph{ordered} prediction rather than a single accuracy comparison. A policy can be perfectly accurate on these tasks while knowing only one way to be right: when $[2,3,4]$ reaches $10$ through both $2\times3+4$ and $3\times4-2$, outcome reward is indifferent between a policy that can produce either mode and one that has collapsed onto a single expression, and the asymmetric negative-advantage gradients of uniform aggregation actively push toward the latter (Section~\ref{sec:where-exploration}). The prediction is therefore: \drgrpo and \method should be roughly comparable on \texttt{pass@1}, \texttt{pass@8}, and \texttt{mean@8}, while \method should retain strictly more of the valid answer set as measured by \texttt{distinct@8} and \texttt{coverage@8}. Coverage gains \emph{with} accuracy losses, or accuracy gains \emph{without} coverage gains, would both contradict the mechanism and will be reported as such. This framing keeps the empirical claim aligned with the mechanism: \method should improve learning when candidate-level exploration creates better within-prompt comparisons, and the regression estimates whether that effect is visible in the completed runs.

Threats to validity and expected failure modes are discussed in
Appendix~\ref{app:threats}.

\paragraph{The pre-registered primary is null.}
At the primary temperature $\tau=0.5$, every outcome is statistically flat
($|\hat\gamma| \le 1.6$ points, all $p \ge 0.14$). We report this first and without
qualification: at the temperature we pre-specified, candidate-level tempering does not
measurably change learning at this scale.

\paragraph{The exploratory low-$\tau$ arms show the pre-registered ordered signature.}
At $\tau=0.05$, \texttt{coverage@8} rises by $+4.8$ points
[$3.6, 5.9$] and \texttt{pass@8} by $+7.9$ points [$4.6, 11.1$] (both Holm-adjusted
$p<10^{-4}$), while \texttt{mean@8} ($-0.8$ points, $p=0.28$) and greedy
\texttt{pass@1} ($+0.9$ points, $p=0.27$) are statistically unchanged, and
\texttt{distinct@8} rises by $+0.24$ modes [$0.18, 0.29$]. This is precisely the
ordered prediction of Section~\ref{sec:interpreting-current-run}: more of the valid
answer set at no per-sample accuracy cost. The effect is monotone toward aggressive
tempering ($\tau=0.25$: coverage $+2.2$ points, Holm $p<10^{-4}$; $\tau=0.1$: $+1.6$,
Holm $p=0.007$; $\tau\ge 0.5$: null) and survives the run-clustered robustness pass at
$\tau=0.05$ (coverage $p<10^{-4}$, \texttt{pass@8} $p=0.0006$). Because these
temperatures were exploratory under our pre-registration, we treat them as
hypothesis-generating and have pre-registered $\tau=0.05$ as the primary for the 3B
replication (Appendix~\ref{app:quartet-settings}).

genuinely different solutions on the prompts that are solved.

diversity via degeneracy rather than via credit assignment.

\section{Code-Faithful Objective Definitions}
\label{app:code-objectives}

This appendix gives the exact training objectives used in the experimental quartet.
$G$ is the number of sampled completions per prompt, $\mathcal A$ the set of prompts in
the batch, $N = |\mathcal A|\,G$ the number of sampled completions in the batch, and
$T_{\max}$ the configured maximum completion length. All four methods share
Appendices~\ref{app:common-rollout} and \ref{app:drgrpo-backend} and differ only in
Appendices~\ref{app:objective-drgrpo}--\ref{app:objective-xdrgrpo}.

\subsection{Common rollout setup}
\label{app:common-rollout}

For each prompt $x$ the rollout policy $\mu$ samples completions
$\{y_{x,1},\dots,y_{x,G}\}$ with scalar rewards $r_{x,i}\in\{0,1\}$ from final-answer
verification. Define the group mean and the centered group-relative advantage
\[
\bar r_x = \frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^G r_{x,i},
\qquad
A_{x,i} = r_{x,i} - \bar r_x
\;\in\;
\Bigl[-\bigl(1-\tfrac1G\bigr),\; 1-\tfrac1G\Bigr].
\]
The implementation also supports standard-deviation scaling,
$A_{x,i} = (r_{x,i}-\bar r_x)/(\operatorname{Std}(\{r_{x,j}\}_j)+10^{-8})$, but this is
disabled in every released recipe (the \texttt{critic\_type}=\texttt{drgrpo} setting
selects the unscaled advantage and the constant-normalizer aggregator below), consistent
with the Dr.GRPO correction \citep{liu2025understanding}.

Let $m_{x,i,t}\in\{0,1\}$ be the completion-token mask, $\log\mu_{x,i,t}$ the rollout
log-probability, and $\log\pi_{\theta,x,i,t}$ the current-policy log-probability. The
tokenwise importance ratio and its clipped version are
\[
c_{x,i,t}
=
\exp\!\bigl(\log \pi_{\theta,x,i,t}-\log \mu_{x,i,t}\bigr),
\qquad
\tilde c_{x,i,t}
=
\operatorname{clip}\bigl(c_{x,i,t},\,1-\epsilon_{\mathrm{low}},\,1+\epsilon_{\mathrm{high}}\bigr).
\]
An optional tokenwise reverse-KL penalty to a frozen reference policy is available:
\[
D_{x,i,t}
=
\exp(\delta_{x,i,t})-\delta_{x,i,t}-1,
\qquad
\delta_{x,i,t}
=
\operatorname{clip}\bigl(
\log \pi_{\mathrm{ref},x,i,t}-\log \pi_{\theta,x,i,t},\,-40,\,40
\bigr),
\]
weighted by $\beta\ge 0$. This is the \emph{only} mechanism in the paper that constrains
$\pi_\theta$ toward $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$; see the warning in Appendix~\ref{app:reference-tilt}.

\subsection{Per-candidate utility and the shared backend}
\label{app:drgrpo-backend}

All four objectives are built from a single per-candidate quantity, the
\emph{normalized Dr.GRPO utility}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:utility-app}
\Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)
:=
\frac{1}{T_{\max}}
\sum_{t}
m_{x,i,t}\,
\min\!\bigl(c_{x,i,t}A_{x,i},\;\tilde c_{x,i,t}A_{x,i}\bigr),
\end{equation}
together with the tokenwise penalty term
$P_{x,i}(\theta) := \tfrac{1}{T_{\max}}\sum_t m_{x,i,t}\,D_{x,i,t}$. The constant
$T_{\max}$ normalizer --- rather than the per-completion length $|y_{x,i}|$ --- is exactly
Dr.GRPO's length-bias correction.

Every method in the quartet then has the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:general-form}
\mathcal L(\theta)
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal A|}
\sum_{x\in\mathcal A}
\sum_{i=1}^G
\Bigl[
-\,\underbrace{a_{x,i}}_{\text{aggregation weight}}\; \Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)
\;+\;
\tfrac{\beta}{G}\,P_{x,i}(\theta)
\Bigr]
\;+\;
\underbrace{R(\theta)}_{\text{optional extra regularizer}} ,
\end{equation}
and the four methods are distinguished entirely by $(a_{x,i}, R)$. This is the axis the
paper studies. Table~\ref{tab:quartet-summary} is the whole quartet in one place.

\subsection{Dr.GRPO}
\label{app:objective-drgrpo}

The baseline sets $a_{x,i} = 1/G$:
\[
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO}}(\theta)
=
-\frac{1}{|\mathcal A|\,G}
\sum_{x\in\mathcal A}\sum_{i=1}^G \Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)
\;+\;
\frac{\beta}{|\mathcal A|\,G}\sum_{x,i} P_{x,i}(\theta)
\;=\;
\frac{1}{N\,T_{\max}}\sum_{x,i,t} m_{x,i,t}
\Bigl[-\min\bigl(c_{x,i,t}A_{x,i},\tilde c_{x,i,t}A_{x,i}\bigr) + \beta D_{x,i,t}\Bigr],
\]
which is the standard implementation-faithful form. The equality is immediate from
$N = |\mathcal A| G$ and \eqref{eq:utility-app}. \emph{The group-relative signal lives
entirely in $A_{x,i}$; the aggregation across the group is a plain mean.}

\subsection{SEED-Dr.GRPO}
\label{app:objective-seed}

SEED preserves the clipped surrogate but rescales each prompt's advantages by semantic
predictive entropy. Let $c(i)$ be the semantic answer cluster of completion $i$ and
\[
\ell_{x,i} = \frac{1}{|y_{x,i}|}\sum_t m_{x,i,t}\log\mu_{x,i,t},
\qquad
p_{x,c} = \frac{\sum_{i:\,c(i)=c}\exp(\ell_{x,i})}{\sum_{j=1}^G \exp(\ell_{x,j})},
\qquad
H^{\mathrm{sem}}_x = -\sum_c p_{x,c}\log p_{x,c}.
\]
We length-normalize $\ell_{x,i}$ (see Appendix~\ref{app:reference-tilt} for why), and
evaluate it at the rollout policy $\mu$, matching the frozen-weight convention of
Remark~\ref{rem:frozen-weights} (at the single update of the released one-epoch recipe,
$\mu = \pi_\theta$). Semantic clusters are the canonical answer modes of the verifier ---
countdown expressions reduce to canonical ASTs, colorings to completion strings --- and
completions with no extractable answer form singleton clusters. The
prompt-level scaling is $s_x = (1+\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}} H^{\mathrm{sem}}_x)^{-1}$ with
$\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}} = \alpha/\log G$, giving $a_{x,i} = s_x/G$. Note that $s_x$ does not
depend on $i$: SEED reweights \emph{prompts}, and remains uniform \emph{within} the group.

\paragraph{Fidelity to, and one departure from, the released SEED-GRPO.} The functional
form above matches the released SEED-GRPO implementation, which likewise scales advantages
by $(1+\alpha' H^{\mathrm{sem}})^{-1}$ with length-normalized sequence likelihoods and the
Shannon entropy of the likelihood-weighted cluster masses (rather than the Monte-Carlo
estimator $-\tfrac1K\sum_k \log p_{c(k)}$ written in that paper), and which also assigns
unparseable completions to singleton clusters \citep{chen2025seedgrpo}. The one deliberate
departure is the sensitivity: the released SEED-GRPO default corresponds to
$\alpha \approx 0.04$ in our parameterization, which caps the prompt rescaling at about
$4\%$ even at maximal semantic entropy --- a perturbation of \drgrpo far below this study's
minimum detectable effect. We instead run the arm at $\alpha = 1$, which reaches
$s_x = \tfrac12$ at maximal entropy, so that the prompt-level mechanism is actually
testable at our sample sizes; results for this arm should be read as testing the
\emph{mechanism at a visible dose}, not the original tuning.

\subsection{Token-MaxEnt Dr.GRPO}
\label{app:objective-token-maxent}

Token-MaxEnt keeps $a_{x,i}=1/G$ and adds an exact next-token entropy bonus with
\[
\mathcal H_{x,i,t}(\pi_\theta)
=
-\sum_{v} \pi_\theta(v\mid x, y_{x,i,<t})\log \pi_\theta(v\mid x, y_{x,i,<t}),
\qquad
R(\theta) = -\frac{\alpha}{|\mathcal A|\,G\,T_{\max}}\sum_{x,i,t} m_{x,i,t}\,\mathcal H_{x,i,t}(\pi_\theta).
\]
This is the classic loss-side policy-entropy bonus \citep{williams1991entropy,mnih2016a3c}
placed on the \drgrpo backbone, with the entropy computed exactly over the vocabulary (no
sampling estimate) and aggregated with the same constant $T_{\max}$ normalizer as the
surrogate. Entropy here acts on the next-token distribution, not on the sampled group.

\subsection{xDr.GRPO}
\label{app:objective-xdrgrpo}

xDr.GRPO changes only $a_{x,i}$. Let $\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x\in\Delta^G$ be the reference
distribution over the sampled candidates (Appendix~\ref{app:reference-tilt}), let $\tau>0$,
$\lambda\ge 0$, and $\kappa := \tau+\lambda$. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xdr-weights-app}
w^\star_{x,i}(\theta)
=
\frac{\exp\bigl(\Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)/\kappa\bigr)\,\bigl(\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,i}\bigr)^{\lambda/\kappa}}
     {\sum_{j=1}^G \exp\bigl(\Ubar_{x,j}(\theta)/\kappa\bigr)\,\bigl(\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,j}\bigr)^{\lambda/\kappa}},
\qquad
a_{x,i} := \mathrm{sg}\bigl[w^\star_{x,i}(\theta)\bigr],
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xdr-loss-app}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{xDr.GRPO}}(\theta)
=
-\frac{1}{|\mathcal A|}\sum_{x\in\mathcal A}\sum_{i=1}^G
\mathrm{sg}\bigl[w^\star_{x,i}\bigr]\;\Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)
\;+\;
\frac{\beta}{|\mathcal A|\,G}\sum_{x,i} P_{x,i}(\theta).
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:xdr-loss-app} is the negative of the log-partition objective
$\mathcal J_{\mathrm{xDr.GRPO}}$ of Section~\ref{sec:where-exploration}; the identification
and the necessity of the $\mathrm{sg}$ are Proposition~\ref{prop:lse-gradient} and
Remark~\ref{rem:detach}.

\paragraph{Diff against the baseline.} The entire implementation delta is:

\begin{verbatim}
    # Utilities at the rollout policy (importance ratio 1): the clipped
    # surrogate reduces to U_i = A_i * T_i / T_max per candidate.
    U = adv * response_token_counts / T_max          # [num_prompts, G]

    # --- Dr.GRPO (tau = inf) ---
    # the reweighting below is skipped entirely; the baseline loss
    # aggregation runs unchanged.

    # --- xDr.GRPO (finite tau) ---
    w = G * torch.softmax(U / tau, dim=-1).detach()  # <-- detach is required
    loss = -(w * U_theta * loss_masks).mean()        # per-row reweighting
\end{verbatim}

\noindent Here $U_\theta$ denotes the tokenwise clipped surrogate recomputed at the
current parameters inside the update loop; only its per-row aggregation weight changes.
Rollouts, rewards, clipping, masking, optimizer, and the tokenwise KL term are untouched.

\begin{remark}[Weights are frozen at the rollout policy]
\label{rem:frozen-weights}
The released implementation computes $w^\star_x$ once per rollout batch from the
utilities evaluated at the rollout policy $\mu$ --- where the importance ratio is $1$ and
$\Ubar_{x,i} = A_{x,i}\,T_{x,i}/T_{\max}$ exactly --- and holds them fixed for the update,
exactly as the advantages themselves are. Under the released recipe (one optimization
epoch, weights consumed at the first gradient step where $\pi_\theta=\mu$) this coincides
with $w^\star(\theta)$; in multi-epoch configurations the two diverge and
$w^\star(\theta)$ would need recomputing per step. Setting $\tau=\infty$ disables the
reweighting entirely, leaving the baseline aggregation \emph{bit-for-bit} unchanged; the
$\tau=\infty$ arm of the sweep in Appendix~\ref{app:tau-range} is run through the same
launcher with the reweighting skipped. The reference tilt ($\lambda>0$,
Appendix~\ref{app:reference-tilt}) is presented for generality; the released
implementation fixes $\lambda=0$.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Degenerate groups.} If all rewards in a group are equal then $A_{x,i}=0$ for all
$i$, hence $\Ubar_{x,i}=0$, $w^\star_x$ is uniform (or purely reference-tilted), and the
gradient of \eqref{eq:xdr-loss-app} vanishes. Uninformative prompts drop out automatically,
matching Dr.GRPO. No prompt-skipping heuristic is used.

\subsection{Shared recipe simplifications}
\label{app:quartet-settings}

All matched recipes use
\[
G=16,\qquad
\texttt{critic\_type}=\texttt{drgrpo},\qquad
\beta=0,\qquad
\lambda=0 ,
\]
with a learning rate of $2\times 10^{-7}$, a training budget of $9{,}216$ prompt
consumptions, one optimization epoch per rollout batch, and sampling temperature $1$.
The group size and learning rate were calibrated on the \drgrpo baseline alone, before
any cross-method comparison: at $G=8$ most groups contain no correct sample at this model
scale (starving the group-relative signal entirely), and at learning rates
$\ge 10^{-6}$ the policy's training success rate collapses toward zero within a run,
independent of method. The remaining pre-specified constants are the Token-MaxEnt
coefficient $\alpha=0.01$, the SEED sensitivity $\alpha=1$, and the primary
xDr.GRPO temperature $\tau=0.5$ (the remaining grid points are reported as
exploratory, with Holm-adjusted $p$-values). For the larger-scale replication
(Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct on the four-number Countdown pool), the primary
temperature is pre-registered as $\tau=0.05$, fixed on the basis of the
0.5B exploratory results before any 3B run was started. With $\beta=0$ the tokenwise KL term
vanishes; with $\lambda=0$ the reference tilt vanishes and $\kappa=\tau$. The comparison
therefore isolates a single axis: the aggregation weight $a_{x,i}$ (uniform for Dr.GRPO,
prompt-rescaled uniform for SEED, uniform-plus-token-entropy for Token-MaxEnt,
tempered-softmax for xDr.GRPO).

\section{Theory}
\label{app:theory}

Throughout, fix a prompt $x$ and abbreviate $\Ubar_i := \Ubar_{x,i}(\theta)$ from
\eqref{eq:utility-app}, $\pi_i := \pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,i}$, $w_i := w_{x,i}$, and
$\kappa := \tau+\lambda > 0$. We assume $\pi_i>0$ for all $i$ and that
$\theta\mapsto \Ubar_i(\theta)$ is differentiable at $\theta$.

\subsection{The reference tilt is an aggregation prior, not a policy trust region}
\label{app:reference-tilt}

The reference distribution over the sampled candidate set is
\[
\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,i}
=
\frac{\exp\bigl(\ell^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,i}\bigr)}{\sum_{j}\exp\bigl(\ell^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,j}\bigr)},
\qquad
\ell^{\mathrm{ref}}_{x,i}
=
\frac{1}{|y_{x,i}|}\sum_t m_{x,i,t}\log \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_{x,i,t}\mid x, y_{x,i,<t}).
\]

\paragraph{Length normalization is not optional here.} An unnormalized sequence
log-likelihood decreases monotonically in completion length, so a softmax over raw
log-likelihood sums places mass on short completions for reasons that have nothing to do
with quality. Since Dr.GRPO's central correction is the removal of exactly this length
bias, a candidate distribution built from raw sums would reintroduce it at the aggregation
step. We therefore length-normalize $\ell^{\mathrm{ref}}$, and likewise $\ell_{x,i}$ in
SEED (Appendix~\ref{app:objective-seed}).

\paragraph{$\lambda$ is not $\beta$.} The term $-\lambda\KL(w\|\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x)$ acts on
the \emph{aggregation weights} $w\in\Delta^G$. It does \emph{not} bound
$\KL(\pi_\theta\|\pi_{\mathrm{ref}})$ and is \emph{not} a trust region on the policy: one can
drive $\pi_\theta$ arbitrarily far from $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$ while $w$ stays pinned to
$\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x$. Trust-region control over the policy, where wanted, is supplied
separately by the tokenwise penalty $\beta D_{x,i,t}$ of
Appendix~\ref{app:common-rollout}. We therefore call $\lambda$ a \emph{reference tilt}: it
biases aggregation toward candidates the base model already finds plausible, damping the
influence of low-likelihood off-policy outliers on the update. It is a prior over
\emph{whom to learn from}, not a leash on \emph{where to go}.

\subsection{Closed-form aggregation weights}

\begin{proposition}[Closed form]
\label{prop:closed-form}
The map $w\mapsto \langle w,\Ubar\rangle + \tau H(w) - \lambda\KL(w\|\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x)$
is strictly concave on $\Delta^G$ and attains its unique maximum at
\[
w^\star_i
=
\frac{\exp(\Ubar_i/\kappa)\,\pi_i^{\lambda/\kappa}}
{\sum_{j=1}^G \exp(\Ubar_j/\kappa)\,\pi_j^{\lambda/\kappa}} .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Collecting the $w_i\log w_i$ terms,
\[
\langle w,\Ubar\rangle + \tau H(w) - \lambda\KL(w\|\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x)
=
\sum_i w_i \Ubar_i
- \kappa\sum_i w_i\log w_i
+ \lambda\sum_i w_i\log \pi_i .
\]
The map $w\mapsto -\sum_i w_i\log w_i$ is strictly concave on the simplex and $\kappa>0$;
the remaining terms are linear in $w$. The objective is therefore strictly concave, so any
stationary point is the unique maximizer. Introducing a multiplier $\eta$ for
$\sum_i w_i = 1$, stationarity reads
$\Ubar_i - \kappa(\log w_i + 1) + \lambda\log\pi_i + \eta = 0$, whence
$w_i \propto \exp(\Ubar_i/\kappa)\,\pi_i^{\lambda/\kappa}$ (the factor
$\exp((\eta-\kappa)/\kappa)$ is common to all coordinates). Normalizing gives the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Log-partition form and gradient]
\label{prop:lse-gradient}
Let $Z := \sum_j \exp(\Ubar_j/\kappa)\,\pi_j^{\lambda/\kappa}$ and let
$\mathcal G_x(\theta)$ denote the optimal value of the objective in
Proposition~\ref{prop:closed-form}. Then
\[
\mathcal G_x(\theta) = \kappa\log Z,
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nabla_\theta \mathcal G_x(\theta)
=
\sum_{i=1}^G w^\star_i(\theta)\,\nabla_\theta \Ubar_i(\theta).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Substituting $w^\star$ and using
$\log w^\star_i = \Ubar_i/\kappa + (\lambda/\kappa)\log\pi_i - \log Z$ into the objective,
the $\Ubar$, entropy, and $\log\pi$ terms cancel pairwise and leave
$\mathcal G_x = \kappa\log Z$. For the gradient, $\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}$ does not depend on
$\theta$, so
\[
\nabla_\theta \mathcal G_x
= \kappa Z^{-1}\nabla_\theta Z
= \kappa Z^{-1}\sum_i \exp(\Ubar_i/\kappa)\,\pi_i^{\lambda/\kappa}\cdot \kappa^{-1}\nabla_\theta \Ubar_i
= \sum_i w^\star_i \,\nabla_\theta \Ubar_i . \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why the weights must be detached]
\label{rem:detach}
Proposition~\ref{prop:lse-gradient} is an envelope (Danskin) statement: although
$w^\star$ depends on $\theta$, that dependence contributes nothing to
$\nabla_\theta\mathcal G_x$, because $w^\star$ is a stationary point of the inner problem.
Consequently the correct implementation of \eqref{eq:xdr-loss-app} treats $w^\star$ as a
constant. Backpropagating through the softmax optimizes a different --- and
uncharacterized --- objective, and is the single easiest way to get this method wrong.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Dr.GRPO is the risk-neutral limit of the family}

\begin{proposition}[Variance expansion]
\label{prop:variance-expansion}
Let $\lambda=0$, and set $\mu_x := \tfrac1G\sum_i \Ubar_i$ and
$\sigma_x^2 := \tfrac1G\sum_i (\Ubar_i - \mu_x)^2$. Then, as $\tau\to\infty$,
\[
\mathcal G_x(\theta)
=
\tau\log G
\;+\;
\underbrace{\mu_x(\theta)}_{\text{Dr.GRPO}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\frac{\sigma_x^2(\theta)}{2\tau}}_{\text{exploration bonus}}
\;+\;
O(\tau^{-2}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Put $\epsilon := 1/\tau$. Then
$\mathcal G_x = \epsilon^{-1}\log\sum_i e^{\epsilon \Ubar_i}
= \epsilon^{-1}\log G + \epsilon^{-1}\log\bigl(\tfrac1G\sum_i e^{\epsilon\Ubar_i}\bigr)$.
The second logarithm is the cumulant generating function of the empirical distribution of
$\{\Ubar_i\}_{i=1}^G$ evaluated at $\epsilon$; since the $\Ubar_i$ are uniformly bounded
(Appendix~\ref{app:tau-range}) it is analytic at $0$ with expansion
$\epsilon\mu_x + \tfrac{\epsilon^2}{2}\sigma_x^2 + O(\epsilon^3)$. Multiplying by
$\epsilon^{-1} = \tau$ gives the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Gradient convergence to Dr.GRPO]
\label{prop:drgrpo-limit}
Let $\lambda=0$, $M_x := \max_i |\Ubar_i(\theta)|$, and
$L_x := \max_i \|\nabla_\theta \Ubar_i(\theta)\|$. Then
\[
\Bigl\|\,
\nabla_\theta \mathcal G_x(\theta)
-
\nabla_\theta \mathcal J_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO},x}(\theta)
\,\Bigr\|
\;\le\;
\bigl(e^{2M_x/\tau} - 1\bigr)\, L_x
\;=\;
O(1/\tau),
\]
where $\mathcal J_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO},x}(\theta) = \tfrac1G\sum_i \Ubar_i(\theta)$ is the
prompt-$x$ contribution to the Dr.GRPO objective
(Appendix~\ref{app:objective-drgrpo}).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:lse-gradient},
$\nabla\mathcal G_x - \nabla\mathcal J_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO},x}
= \sum_i \bigl(w^\star_i - \tfrac1G\bigr)\nabla\Ubar_i$, so by the triangle inequality the
norm is at most $\|w^\star - \mathbf u\|_1 L_x$ with $\mathbf u$ uniform. Apply
Proposition~\ref{prop:stability} to the pair $(\Ubar,\,0)$, noting that with $\lambda=0$ the
weights induced by the all-zero utility vector are exactly $\mathbf u$, and that
$\|\Ubar - 0\|_\infty = M_x$. This gives
$\bigl|\log(w^\star_i/\tfrac1G)\bigr| \le 2M_x/\tau$ for every $i$, hence
$\bigl|w^\star_i - \tfrac1G\bigr| \le \tfrac1G\bigl(e^{2M_x/\tau}-1\bigr)$ and, summing,
$\|w^\star - \mathbf u\|_1 \le e^{2M_x/\tau}-1$. Finally $e^{2M_x/\tau}-1 = O(1/\tau)$ for
bounded $M_x$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Optimism]
\label{cor:optimism}
For every $\tau>0$ with $\lambda=0$,
\[
\mathcal G_x(\theta) - \tau\log G
\;\ge\;
\mathcal J_{\mathrm{Dr.GRPO},x}(\theta),
\]
with equality if and only if $\Ubar_1 = \dots = \Ubar_G$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$\tau\log\bigl(\tfrac1G\sum_i e^{\Ubar_i/\tau}\bigr) \ge \tfrac1G\sum_i \Ubar_i$ by Jensen's
inequality applied to the strictly convex map $u\mapsto e^{u/\tau}$; equality holds iff
$e^{\Ubar_i/\tau}$ is constant in $i$, i.e.\ iff the $\Ubar_i$ coincide.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[What the theory actually says]
\label{rem:interpretation}
Read together, Propositions~\ref{prop:variance-expansion}--\ref{prop:drgrpo-limit} and
Corollary~\ref{cor:optimism} give the paper's claim its precise form.
\emph{(i)} Dr.GRPO is not ``xDr.GRPO without exploration'': it is xDr.GRPO at
$\tau=\infty$, the risk-neutral corner of a one-parameter family. The uniform aggregation
in Dr.GRPO is an unexamined default, not a derived quantity.
\emph{(ii)} Finite $\tau$ replaces the arithmetic mean of candidate utilities by their
tempered log-sum-exp --- the \emph{entropic risk measure} --- which by
Corollary~\ref{cor:optimism} upper-bounds the Dr.GRPO objective by exactly the within-group
\emph{dispersion} of candidate utilities. Maximizing that bound rewards groups whose
candidates still disagree. This is optimism in the face of uncertainty, applied to the
object GRPO uses for credit assignment.
\emph{(iii)} Mechanically, $w^\star_{x,i}\propto\exp(\Ubar_{x,i}/\tau)$ and $\Ubar_{x,i}$ is
increasing in $A_{x,i}$, so negative-advantage candidates receive exponentially attenuated
gradients. The theory establishes the attenuation, not its downstream effect on policy
entropy: the token-level literature attributes entropy collapse variously to
high-covariance positive-advantage updates \citep{cui2025entropy} --- with sequence-level
negative reinforcement alone even \emph{preserving} entropy \citep{zhu2025negative} --- and
to negative gradients on low-probability tokens
\citep{park2025cliplow,huang2025lowprob,deng2025negativegrad}. Whether candidate-level
attenuation sustains a higher effective rollout count and a lower incorrect-mass share is
what Figure~\ref{fig:exploration-sidecar} is designed to measure.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Stability under utility perturbation}

The utilities $\Ubar_i$ are computed from noisy verifier rewards (and, in multi-epoch
configurations, drifting importance ratios), so the aggregation weights must not be brittle
in them. They are not: the entropy
weight controls the Lipschitz constant directly.

\begin{proposition}[Coordinatewise stability]
\label{prop:stability}
Let $\Ubar,\widetilde\Ubar \in \mathbb R^G$ with
$\|\widetilde\Ubar - \Ubar\|_\infty \le \varepsilon$, and let $w^\star,\widetilde w^\star$ be
the corresponding weights from Proposition~\ref{prop:closed-form} under the same
$\tau,\lambda,\pi^{\mathrm{ref}}_x$. Then for every $i$,
\[
\Bigl|\log \tfrac{\widetilde w^\star_i}{w^\star_i}\Bigr| \;\le\; \frac{2\varepsilon}{\kappa},
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\bigl|\widetilde{\mathcal G}_x - \mathcal G_x\bigr| \;\le\; \varepsilon .
\]
\end{proposition}

%% NOTE TO AUTHORS: the imported source was truncated in a small gap
%% after the statement of Proposition~\ref{prop:stability}. Two pieces
%% in that gap are reconstructions, not original text: (1) the proof of
%% Proposition~\ref{prop:stability} immediately below, and (2) the
%% boundedness / $\tau$-range subsection carrying the label
%% app:tau-range (that label is referenced from the main text and from
%% the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:variance-expansion}, but no
%% section carrying it appeared in the provided source).
%% Figure~\ref{fig:exploration-sidecar} in Appendix C is likewise a
%% reconstructed placeholder. Everything else from here on is the
%% authors' original text.
%% ====================================================================

\begin{proof}
Write $Z = \sum_j \exp(\Ubar_j/\kappa)\,\pi_j^{\lambda/\kappa}$ and
$\widetilde Z$ for its counterpart under $\widetilde\Ubar$. Since
$|\widetilde\Ubar_j - \Ubar_j|\le\varepsilon$ for every $j$, each summand of
$\widetilde Z$ is within a factor $e^{\pm\varepsilon/\kappa}$ of the corresponding
summand of $Z$, hence
$e^{-\varepsilon/\kappa} \le \widetilde Z / Z \le e^{\varepsilon/\kappa}$, i.e.\
$|\log(\widetilde Z/Z)|\le \varepsilon/\kappa$. For the weights,
\[
\log\frac{\widetilde w^\star_i}{w^\star_i}
=
\frac{\widetilde\Ubar_i - \Ubar_i}{\kappa} - \log\frac{\widetilde Z}{Z},
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
\Bigl|\log\frac{\widetilde w^\star_i}{w^\star_i}\Bigr|
\le \frac{\varepsilon}{\kappa} + \frac{\varepsilon}{\kappa}
= \frac{2\varepsilon}{\kappa}.
\]
For the objective values, Proposition~\ref{prop:lse-gradient} gives
$\widetilde{\mathcal G}_x - \mathcal G_x = \kappa\log(\widetilde Z/Z)$, whose absolute
value is at most $\kappa\cdot\varepsilon/\kappa = \varepsilon$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Boundedness of the utilities and the practical range of $\tau$}
\label{app:tau-range}

\paragraph{Boundedness.}
The utilities that enter the aggregation weights are evaluated at the rollout policy
(Remark~\ref{rem:frozen-weights}), where the importance ratio is exactly $1$ and every
summand of \eqref{eq:utility-app} equals $A_{x,i}$ on active positions. Since the mask has
at most $T_{\max}$ active positions and $|A_{x,i}|\le 1-\tfrac1G$,
\[
M_x \;=\; \max_i\,\bigl|\Ubar_{x,i}\bigr| \;=\; \max_i\, |A_{x,i}|\,\frac{T_{x,i}}{T_{\max}}
\;\le\; 1-\frac1G
\]
exactly, with no clipping argument required. (For the general current-$\theta$ surrogate
the positive branch is still bounded by $(1+\epsilon_{\mathrm{high}})|A_{x,i}|$, but the
unclipped negative branch is unbounded in principle; the released weights never evaluate
it off-policy.) This uniform boundedness is what
Proposition~\ref{prop:variance-expansion} uses, and it justifies treating $[-1,1]$ as the
utility scale when choosing $\tau$.

\paragraph{Practical range of $\tau$.}
The weights depend on the utilities only through $\Ubar_{x,i}/\kappa$, so the meaningful
scale for $\tau$ is the within-group utility spread, which for binary rewards and $G=16$ is
of order $10^{-1}$ after the $T_{\max}$ normalization. The sweep uses the grid
$\tau \in \{0.05, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, \infty\}$ with $\lambda=0$
(Table~\ref{tab:matched-run-design}), where the $\tau=\infty$ arm disables the reweighting
entirely and runs the identical baseline code path
(Remark~\ref{rem:frozen-weights}), reproducing Dr.GRPO bit-for-bit. Values of $\tau$ far below the utility spread approach the best-of-$G$ corner
and inherit its instability (Proposition~\ref{prop:stability}); values far above it are
indistinguishable from Dr.GRPO by Proposition~\ref{prop:drgrpo-limit}. Reported $\tau$
values should be read relative to the utility scale $M_x\le 1$ above.

\paragraph{Objective-specific difference.}
Dr.GRPO aggregates per-candidate utilities uniformly within each prompt group. xDr.GRPO
aggregates the \emph{same} utilities with the tempered softmax weights of
\eqref{eq:xdr-weights-app}, computed at the rollout policy and frozen per rollout batch
(Remark~\ref{rem:frozen-weights}). SEED-Dr.GRPO
rescales whole prompts by semantic entropy; Token-MaxEnt Dr.GRPO adds a next-token entropy
bonus. Nothing else changes.

\paragraph{Diagnostics.}
The candidate-level diagnostics are defined for every method through the realized
aggregation weights $a_{x,i}$ of \eqref{eq:general-form}: the effective number of active
rollouts $\exp\bigl(H(a_x)\bigr)$, with $a_x$ normalized within the prompt group over the
rows that train, and the share of aggregation mass falling on incorrect rollouts
$\sum_{i:\,r_{x,i}=0} a_{x,i}$. Because Dr.GRPO also has an explicit $a_{x,i}$ under
\eqref{eq:general-form} (namely $1/G$), the training loop computes both quantities
identically for every arm at every step --- uniform weights for Dr.GRPO and Token-MaxEnt,
the prompt-rescaled uniform weights for SEED (whose within-group distribution is uniform,
so its curves coincide with the baseline's by construction), and the tempered softmax
weights for xDr.GRPO --- and logs them under a shared key, so the curves are directly
comparable. Figure~\ref{fig:exploration-sidecar} plots exactly these quantities.

\section{Threats to Validity and Expected Failure Modes}
\label{app:threats}

Several threats could limit the interpretation of the proposed comparison. First, although the verifier is exact --- expressions are evaluated with rational arithmetic and colorings are checked against the enumerated valid set, so false positives are essentially ruled out --- the strict answer-extraction gate can produce false negatives on malformed but semantically correct outputs. We will therefore inspect a sample of rejected completions from each domain and report robustness to stricter and more permissive extraction rules. Relatedly, what counts as a \emph{distinct} mode is a canonicalization convention (commutative operands are merged; operator identity is preserved), and coverage results should be read relative to that convention. Second, the study could overstate the effect of xDr.GRPO if the two methods receive unequal hyperparameter tuning or if checkpoint selection is based on the same evaluation splits used for final reporting. To reduce this risk, all main runs will use a matched training budget, shared evaluation harness, and a pre-specified checkpoint-selection rule. Third, \texttt{pass@8} and \texttt{coverage@8} may reward diversity without necessarily reflecting better single-solution reasoning, while \texttt{mean@8} may penalize exploratory methods that produce one strong answer and several weak alternatives. We therefore interpret improvements jointly across \texttt{pass@1}, \texttt{pass@8}, \texttt{mean@8}, and \texttt{coverage@8}, and use the single-mode control split as a guardrail that single-answer performance is preserved. Finally, as the current experiment uses a relatively small 0.5B model on synthetic tasks whose full answer sets are enumerable, a positive effect should be interpreted as evidence that candidate-level exploration changes the learning signal under this controlled setting, not as a claim that xDr.GRPO will dominate at larger scales, on natural reasoning corpora, or under different reward models. In cases where the method improves sampled metrics but not greedy accuracy, or where gains appear only in one task domain, we will report this as a
failure mode rather than as broad evidence of superior reasoning.
